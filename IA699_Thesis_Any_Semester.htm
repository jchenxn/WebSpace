1. Course description: IA 699 is a two-semester, faculty-supervised thesis course that serves as the culminating experience for the Master of Science in Information Assurance. The course provides students with the opportunity to conduct independent, original, and rigorous research in an approved area of information assurance or cybersecurity. Students are expected to apply advanced theoretical knowledge, research methodologies, and analytical skills to investigate a significant problem, contribute to the academic or professional body of knowledge, and demonstrate mastery of graduate-level scholarship. The thesis process culminates in a formal written thesis and an oral defense before a faculty committee.                                  .

2. Learning Outcomes:  Upon successful completion of IA 699, students will be able to: 1. Formulate a clear, focused, and researchable problem in the field of information assurance or cybersecurity. 2. Conduct an extensive literature review using peer-reviewed scholarly sources. 3. Apply appropriate qualitative, quantitative, or mixed research methodologies. 4. Analyze and interpret research findings using sound analytical and critical-thinking skills. 5. Produce a well-organized, technically sound, and academically rigorous thesis that meets graduate-level standards. 6. Demonstrate oral communication skills through a formal thesis proposal defense and final thesis defense. 7. Adhere to ethical research practices, academic integrity standards, and proper scholarly citation conventions.

3. Required Textbook: None  

4. Course Pre-requisites
Enrollment in IA 699 requires: (1). Approval by the Thesis Committee Chairperson. (2). Formation of an approved thesis committee. (3). Good academic standing with a minimum cumulative GPA of 3.0. (4). Completion of all required core coursework or concurrent completion as approved by the Chairperson.

5. Requirements 

Thesis Proposal (First Semester)
A. Students must develop a formal written thesis proposal, including problem statement, literature review, methodology, and preliminary references.
B. A proposal defense is required at least three (3) weeks before the end of the semester during the first thesis semester.
C. The student is responsible for contacting the Chairperson and committee members to schedule the proposal defense.

Thesis Completion and Final Defense (Second Semester)
A. Students must complete the full written thesis in accordance with departmental and university guidelines.
B. A final oral defense must be held at least three (3) weeks before the end of the semester.
C. The final defense may be conducted in person or online (via Zoom), subject to committee approval.
D. The student is responsible for coordinating scheduling with the Chairperson and committee members.

6. Grading
IA 699 is graded on a Satisfactory (S) / Unsatisfactory (U) basis. S (Satisfactory): Student demonstrates acceptable progress and/or successful completion of thesis requirements for the semester. U (Unsatisfactory): Student fails to demonstrate adequate progress or does not meet required milestones. A grade of S is required in each semester to remain enrolled in the thesis sequence.

7. Academic Integrity and Use of AI Tools
Students are expected to adhere to the highest standards of academic integrity. AI-generated content is prohibited in the thesis unless carefully reviewed, verified, and explicitly used as a support tool rather than as an authoring substitute. Students are fully responsible for the accuracy, originality, and scholarly integrity of all submitted work. All sources must be properly cited using an approved academic citation style.

 

8. Citation and Research Standars


A.  A minimum of 15 peer-reviewed research sources must be cited in the thesis.
B. At least 90% of all references must be peer-reviewed scholarly publications (e.g., academic journals, conference proceedings).
C. Non-peer-reviewed sources (e.g., white papers, technical reports) may be used sparingly and must be clearly justified.

Violations of academic integrity policies may result in failure of the course and additional university sanctions.
