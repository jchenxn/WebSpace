DESCRIPTION This course introduces students to Object-Oriented / Event-driven programming. Students will gain hands-on experience in developing professional-look business applications in a visual programming environment. The class will involve lecture, demonstration, discussion, and hands-on exercises. The topics covered include, but not limited to: objects, properties, methods, and events, graphical user interface design, three program control structures, sequential and random file access, data validation and error trapping, database access, functions and procedures, array, and program packaging and deployment.

REQUIRED TEXT: Philip A. Koneman, Visual Basic.NET -- Programming for Business, Prentice Hall, 2003. ISBN 0-13-047368-5.

REFERENCE TEXT: Ekedahl and Newman, Visual Basic.NET: An Object-Oriented Approach, Course Technology, 2003. ISBN 0-619-0158-2.

Programming Projects
There will be no more than three (3) individual programming assignments and two (2) group programming projects. Programs must be dropped in Y drive under the appropriate folder for this class at the beginning of class on the due date. A paper copy of each program with an attached Project Evaluation Form is also required (10 points off if a paper copy of your program or the Project Evaluation Form is not submitted). The penalty for late programs is 50 percent (1/2) of total points off for each day late. All programs should be internally documented ( 3 points off if no openning documentation). Documentation includes the purpose of the program and procedures, your name, date, comments on variables and algorithm, etc.

Exams & Quizzes
Examinations are scheduled as noted in the course calendar, or announced in class and must be taken as scheduled unless a special arrangement is made with the professor before the exam. The examinations will cover those chapters covered since the previous test (not cumulative). A typical exam consists of mostly multiple choice questions and a few short answer type questions.

Quizzes will be announced at least one day before they are given. If attendance or tardiness becomes a problem, the instructor reserves the right to revise the policy and give unannunced quizzes. No makeup quizzes are allowed without officially documented excuse (e.g doctor's letter). All quizzes are about 15 - 20 minutes short answer or problem sloving type, and worth 10 to 20 points each.

Grade Appeals Policy
It is the students' responsibility to keep a record of all grades he or she earned on projects, quizzes, and exams. (The professor's record of your grades will be assumed to be accurate unless you can prove otherwise). Any student wishing to appeal a grade must submit a WRITTEN appeal including a complete explanation (rationale) of why the student feels they deserve a different grade. Verbal grade appeals will not be accepted.

Course Grades
Your grade in this course will be determined by your individual and group performance. The following scale is used to assign letter grades: 90-100% = A, 80-89% = B, 70-79% = C, 60-69% = D, < 60% = F. The instructor reserves the right to adjust this scale.

ACADEMIC INTEGRITY
Cheating in any form will not be tolerated. Any student who cheats on programming projects or during an examination or assists another student in cheating will automatically fail the course. Several students in the past have failed the class or been on academic probation as the result of cheating on programming projects. You are allowed to discuss ideas and problem solving strategies with you classmates. You are NOT allowed to copy the design or actual code from classmates. Unusally similar programs attract intense scrutiny. Once a program comes under suspicion, the student will be monitored closely. All cases of cheating will be reported to the Vice President of Student Life and Development.
PROGRAMMING TIPS
Some students find that programming courses require a significant investment of time. Planning your work carefully, however, can minimize the time required. Students, who procrastinate, and/or try to complete their assignments without understanding the specifications or algorithms, will find the time required excessive and the work very difficult. If you have your assignments done a day or two early, life will be a lot easier and more pleasant.

You will submit your program via network drive Y. Detailed instructions on accessing Y drive will be given to you later. You may need a flash memory or a few floppy disks to backup your solution files regularly. You can also use HuskyNet file space as backup location. Each VB.NET program consists of many files with subdirectories, organized under a single "Solution" folder. Therefore, when making backup, you need to be very careful not to miss any file. This can be best done by copying the entire VB.NET solution folder using Microsoft Explorer.
